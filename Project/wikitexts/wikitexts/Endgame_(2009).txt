The film is produced by [[Daybreak Pictures]] and reunites Travis with ''[[Vantage Point (film)|Vantage Point]]'' actor [[William Hurt]]. It also stars [[Chiwetel Ejiofor]], [[Jonny Lee Miller]] and [[Mark Strong]]. The film dramatises the final days of [[apartheid in South Africa]]. It was filmed at locations in [[Reading, Berkshire|Reading]] in [[England]] and [[Cape Town]], [[South Africa]] in the first half of 2008 and was completed in December that year.\n\nThe film had its world premiere on 18 January 2009 at the [[Sundance Film Festival]] and was broadcast on [[Channel 4]] on 4 May 2009. It also had an international theatrical release, the distribution of which was handled by Target Entertainment Group.\n\n==Plot==\nThe film depicts the final days of [[apartheid]], focusing on secret talks held between the [[African National Congress]] and the members of the [[National Party (South Africa)|National Party]] in a country house in [[Somerset]], [[England]].<ref name=\"Carnevale\">Carnevale, Rob (5 March 2008). \"[http://www.indielondon.co.uk/Film-Review/vantage-point-pete-travis-interview Vantage Point - Pete Travis interview]\". IndieLondon. Retrieved on 17 April 2008.</ref> The film focuses on the relationship that develops between [[Willie Esterhuyse]] and [[Thabo Mbeki]].<ref name=\"Thorpe\"/>\n\nThe secret talks were brokered by [[Michael Young (businessman)|Michael Young]], a British businessman who worked for [[Consolidated Gold Fields]], a firm with considerable interests in South Africa. The talks took place in [[Mells Park House]], a country house near [[Frome]] in Somerset. The house was then owned by Consolidated Gold Fields.\n\nConsolidated Gold Fields was a company with interests in South Africa which is the subject of sanctions by other nations. In one scene, Young and Rudolf Agnew, chairman of Consolidated Gold Fields, leave their offices in London and are mobbed by anti-apartheid protesters who batter and chase their car, unaware that the two men are sponsoring the very talks that are leading to the end of the system they oppose.\n\nIn an interview on BBC Radio 4's ''Today'' programme on 24 April 2009, Michael Young mentioned how he had been asked by Thabo Mbeki to write the final chapter of the 2003 book by Robert Harvey on the ''Fall of Apartheid'', the chapter titled \"Endgame\", on which this film is based.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b006qj9z/episodes/player|title=Today - Available now - BBC Radio 4|website=BBC|language=en-GB|access-date=2018-06-18}}</ref><ref>http://us.macmillan.com/thefallofapartheid {{Dead link|date=February 2022}}</ref>\n\n==Cast==\n* [[William Hurt]] as [[Willie Esterhuyse]], professor of philosophy at [[Stellenbosch University]]\n* [[Chiwetel Ejiofor]] as [[Thabo Mbeki]], director of information for the African National Congress\n* [[Jonny Lee Miller]] as [[Michael Young (businessman)|Michael Young]], director of communications for Consolidated Gold Fields\n* [[Mark Strong]] as [[Niel Barnard]], head of the [[National Intelligence Service (South Africa)|National Intelligence Service]]\n* [[Derek Jacobi]] as [[Rudolph Agnew]], chairman of Consolidated Gold Fields\n* [[Timothy West]] as [[P.W. Aukin had previously produced the acclaimed political drama ''[[The Government Inspector (film)|The Government Inspector]]''.<ref name=\"Thorpe\"/>\n\nAukin and his production partner Hal Vogel contacted Paula Milne to write the script. She spent 18 months on the screenplay and researched the history of the talks by speaking to Thabo Mbeki and Michael Young in South Africa.<ref name=\"Vogel\">Vogel, Hal (7 April 2009). \"[http://www.broadcastnow.co.uk/hotshotspostpro/2009/04/on_location_endgame.html On Location: Endgame]\". Travis was not interested in directing a historical drama about recent events and decided to turn the film into a political thriller.<ref name=\"Kemp2\">Kemp, Stuart (4 November 2008). \"[http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/hr/content_display/features/interviews_profiles/e3i50e90e3361925e28f8b984dce5db9436 Q&A: Pete Travis]\".
