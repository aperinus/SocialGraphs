\n}}\n'''''Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Price''''' is a 2005 [[documentary film]] by director [[Robert Greenwald]] and [[Brave New Films]].<ref name=rottentomatoes /> The film presents a [[Criticism of Walmart|negative picture of Walmart's business practices]] through interviews with former employees, small business owners, and footage of Walmart executives.<ref name=bostonglobe />  Greenwald also uses statistics interspersed between interview footage, to provide an objective analysis of the effects Walmart has on individuals and communities.<ref name=laramie>{{cite web | last =Roten | first =Robert | title = Laramie Movie Scope: Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Prices | date =20 November 2005 | url = http://www.lariat.org/AtTheMovies/new/walmart.html }}</ref>\n\n==Synopsis==\nThe film features archival footage of Walmart CEO [[Lee Scott (businessman)|Lee Scott]] praising the corporation at a large employee convention, intercut with interviews designed to undercut Scott's statements.<ref name=ViewLondon /><ref name=tvguide/>\n\nThe documentary argues that Walmart underpays its workers, paying them an average of $17,000 per year (in 2005 dollars).<ref name=spirituality>{{cite web | last =Brussat | first =Frederic & Mary Ann | title =Film Review | publisher =Spirituality and Practice | date = 4 December 2005 | url = http://www.spiritualityandpractice.com/films/films.php?id=10188 }}</ref> According to the interviews, these wages are too low for employees to afford Walmart's health insurance, so management counsels workers to apply for government programs such as [[Medicaid]] instead.<ref name=bostonglobe/> Greenwald also claims that Walmart hires [[undocumented workers]] for their cleanup crews, paying them well below minimum wage.<ref name=rottentomatoes/> Other criticisms of the retail mega-chain include Walmart's anti-[[trade union|union]] practices, its negative effect on [[mom and pop]] stores and small communities, insufficient environmental protection policies, and its poor record on [[worker's rights]] in the [[United States]] and internationally.<ref name=oldschool>{{cite web | last =Nesbit | first =John | title = Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Price (2005) | publisher =Old School Reviews | date =26 January 2006 | url = http://oldschoolreviews.com/rev_2000/walmart.htm }}</ref> Scenes filmed abroad document factory workers in [[Bangladesh]] and [[China]] creating Walmart goods for as little as 18 cents an hour.<ref name=spirituality/> One 9-year veteran of Walmart testifies that he was moved to tears when he viewed the conditions in clothing manufacturing facilities in [[Latin America]]. He reported the abuses but the company did not correct them.<ref name=latimes>{{cite web | last =Turan | first =Kenneth | title = 'Wal-Mart' seen through the eyes of the disaffected| work = Los Angeles Times | date = 4 November 2005 | url = http://articles.latimes.com/2005/nov/04/entertainment/et-walmart4 }}</ref> The documentary also argues that Walmart's parking lots have unusually high crime rates, a situation that could be vastly improved if the company were willing to spend the money to place cameras outside the stores.<ref name=laramie/>\n\nTo avoid accusations of a partisan POV, most of Greenwald's interviews are of politically conservative, patriotic, \"[[red state]]\" citizens who are distressed about Walmart's policies and impact.<ref name=tvguide/><ref name=latimes/><ref name=variety>{{cite web | last =Anderson | first =John | title = Review: 'Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Price' | publisher = Variety | date = 3 November 2005 | url = https://variety.com/2005/film/reviews/wal-mart-the-high-cost-of-low-price-1200520506/ }}</ref>\n\nAs the film draws to a close, Greenwald documents the efforts of several communities that have successfully blocked Walmarts from opening in their towns, suggesting that others should do the same.<ref name=spirituality/>\n\n==Reception==\n\n===Critical response===\n''Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Price'' has enjoyed critical acclaim and earned a 93% on [[Rotten Tomatoes]] based on reviews from 28 critics.<ref name=rottentomatoes /> On [[Metacritic]] the film has a score of 71 out of 100 based on reviews from 18 critics.<ref>{{Metacritic film|title=Wal-Mart: The High Cost of Low Price}}</ref>\n\nTy Burr of ''[[The Boston Globe]]'' calls it \"advocacy journalism at its most unsparing, and it demands to be seen, discussed, argued with, and acted upon.\"<ref name=bostonglobe /> Matthew Turner of ''The ViewLondon'' reviews says, \"If Greenwald\u2019s intention was to make the audience very angry indeed then the film is a resounding success.\"<ref name=\"ViewLondon\"/>\n\nThe film has been endorsed and promoted by [[MoveOn|MoveOn.org]]; unions, through the [[Wake Up Wal-Mart]] and [[Walmart Watch]] campaigns; and other groups.{{citation needed|date=August 2022}} Some reviewers have observed that while the documentary features stories of former employees and residents of
