International Box Office Results - Box Office Mojo|website=www.boxofficemojo.com}}</ref>\n}}\n\n'''''Grey Gardens''''' is a 1975 American [[documentary film]] by [[Albert and David Maysles]]. The film depicts the everyday lives of two [[Recluse|reclusive]], [[upper class|upper-class]] women, a mother and daughter both named Edith Beale, who lived in poverty at [[Grey Gardens (estate)|Grey Gardens]], a derelict mansion at 3 West End Road in the wealthy [[Georgica Pond]] neighborhood of [[East Hampton (town), New York|East Hampton]], New York. The film was screened at the [[1976 Cannes Film Festival]] but was not entered into the main competition.<ref name=\"festival-cannes.com\">{{cite web |url=http://www.festival-cannes.com/en/archives/ficheFilm/id/2151/year/1976.html |title=Festival de Cannes: Grey Gardens |access-date=2009-05-10|work=festival-cannes.com}}</ref>\n\nEllen Hovde and [[Muffie Meyer]] also directed, and [[Susan Froemke]] was the associate producer. The film's editors are credited as Hovde (who also edited ''[[Gimme Shelter (1970 film)|Gimme Shelter]]'' and ''[[Salesman (1969 film)|Salesman]]''), Meyer and Froemke.<ref>{{Official website|http://mayslesfilms.com/film/grey-gardens/}}</ref>\n\nIn 2010, the film was selected by the [[Library of Congress]] for preservation in the United States [[National Film Registry]] as being \"culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant\".<ref>{{Cite web|title=Hollywood Blockbusters, Independent Films and Shorts Selected for 2010 National Film Registry|url=https://www.loc.gov/item/prn-10-273/2010-national-film-registry-announced/2010-12-28/|access-date=2020-10-13|website=Library of Congress}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=Complete National Film Registry Listing |url=https://www.loc.gov/programs/national-film-preservation-board/film-registry/complete-national-film-registry-listing/|access-date=2020-10-13|website=Library of Congress}}</ref>\n\n==Cast==\n* [[Edith Ewing Bouvier Beale|Edith \"Big Edie\" Ewing Bouvier Beale]] as Herself\n* [[Edith Bouvier Beale|Edith \"Little Edie\" Bouvier Beale]] as Herself\n* Brooks Hyers as Himself\u2014Gardener\n* [[Norman Vincent Peale]] as Himself (voice)\n* Jack Helmuth as Himself\u2014Birthday Guest (uncredited)\n* [[Albert Maysles]] as Himself (uncredited)\n* [[David Maysles]] as Himself (uncredited)\n* [[Jerry Torre]] as Himself\u2014Handyman (uncredited)\n* [[Lois Wright]] as Herself\u2014Birthday Guest (uncredited)\n\n==Background==\n[[File:Eh-village-map.gif|thumb|Map of [[East Hampton (village), New York|East Hampton]]; the Beales lived at Georgica Pond in the southwest of the [[Administrative divisions of New York#Village|village]].]]\n[[Edith Ewing Bouvier Beale]] (1895\u20131977), known as \"Big Edie\", and her daughter [[Edith Bouvier Beale]] (1917\u20132002), known as \"Little Edie\", were the aunt and the first cousin, respectively, of former US First Lady [[Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis]]. The two women lived together at the [[Grey Gardens (estate)|Grey Gardens estate]] for decades with limited funds in increasing squalor and isolation.<ref>{{cite news|title=The top houses from the movies| url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/property/propertypicturegalleries/9239346/The-top-houses-from-the-movies.html| archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120502235333/http://www.telegraph.co.uk/property/propertypicturegalleries/9239346/The-top-houses-from-the-movies.html| url-status=dead| archive-date=May 2, 2012| newspaper=Daily Telegraph | location=London|date=May 2, 2012}}</ref>\n\nThe house was designed in 1897 by Joseph Greenleaf Thorpe and purchased in 1923 by \"Big Edie\" and her husband [[Phelan Beale]]. After Phelan left his wife, \"Big Edie\" and \"Little Edie\" lived there for more than 50 years. The house was called Grey Gardens because of the color of the dunes, the concrete garden walls, and the sea mist.<ref>{{citation | url=http://www.easthamptonhistory.org/events/greygardens08-info2.html| archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090422050405/http://www.easthamptonhistory.org/events/greygardens08-info2.html| archive-date=2009-04-22| access-date=October 13, 2013|title=A Return to Grey Gardens}}</ref>\n\nThroughout the fall of 1971 and into 1972, their living conditions\u2014their house was infested by fleas, inhabited by numerous cats and raccoons, deprived of running water, and filled with garbage and decay\u2014were exposed as the result of an article in the ''[[National Enquirer]]'' and a cover story in ''[[New York (magazine)|New York Magazine]]''<ref>{{cite magazine|last=Sheehy| first=Gail| title=The Secret Of Grey Gardens | magazine =New York Magazine | url =https://nymag.com/news/features/56102/}} Retrieved on 20 May 2014.</ref> after a series of inspections (which the Beales called \"raids\") by the [[Suffolk County, New York|Suffolk County]] Health Department. With the Beale women facing eviction and the razing of their house, in the summer of 1972 Jacqueline Onassis and her sister [[Lee Radziwill]] provided the necessary funds to stabilize and repair the dilapidated house so that it would meet village codes.{{citation needed|date=October 2021}}\n\nAlbert and David Maysles became interested in their story and received permission to film a documentary about the women, which was released in 1976 to wide critical acclaim. Their ''[[direct cinema]]'' technique left the women to tell their own stories.<ref name=\"Webb\">{{cite web|url=https://www.thewhitereview.org/feature/film-direct-cinema-grey-gardens-summer/|title=It\u2019s All In The Film: Direct Cinema, \u2018Grey Gardens\u2019 and \u2018That Summer\u2019|author=Ralf Webb|date=September 2018|website=The White Review}}</ref>\n\n==Production==\n[[Albert and David Maysles]] came into contact with the Beales in 1972 after [[Lee Radziwill]] suggested they make a documentary on her childhood in [[East Hampton (town), New York|East Hampton]] and took them with her on a trip to Grey Gardens. The initial film was funded by Radziwill but was eventually shelved and the footage was lost. The Maysles brothers returned in 1974 without Radziwill's support to film ''Grey Gardens''.<ref name=\"Webb\"/>\n\n==Soundtrack==\n* Edith Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"[[Tea for Two (song)|Tea for Two]]\" (music by [[Vincent Youmans]] and lyrics by [[Irving Caesar]])\n* Edith Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"We Belong Together\" from ''[[Music in the Air]]'' (lyrics and book by [[Oscar Hammerstein II]] and music by [[Jerome Kern]])\n* Edith Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"[[You and the Night and the Music]]\" (music by [[Arthur Schwartz]] and lyrics by [[Howard Dietz]])\n* Edith Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"[[Night and Day (song)|Night and Day]]\" (written by [[Cole Porter]])\n* Edith \"Little Edie\" Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"[[People Will Say We're in Love]]\" (music by [[Richard Rodgers]] and [[Oscar Hammerstein II]])\n* Edith \"Little Edie\" Bouvier Beale&nbsp;\u2013 \"[[Lili Marleen]]\"\n\n==Aftermath==\n\"Big Edie\" died in 1977 and \"Little Edie\" sold the house in 1979 for $220,000 ({{Inflation|US|220000|1979|r=-3|fmt=eq}}) to [[Sally Quinn]] and her husband, longtime ''[[Washington Post]]'' editor [[Ben Bradlee]],<ref>{{cite magazine | url=https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2014/12/15/let-go | title=Let it Go | first=Joan | last=Acocella | magazine=[[The New Yorker]] | date=December 15, 2014}}</ref> who promised to restore the dilapidated structure (the sale agreement forbade razing the house). For the next several years, the property was available to rent until Quinn ultimately sold it in 2017. Prior to the sale, Quinn was forced to sell the remaining furniture originally belonging to the Beales, citing a lack of space. As of 2017, fashion designer [[Liz Lange]] is the owner of Grey Gardens. Lange and her husband have extensively remodelled the house, including lifting the house on stilts to create a basement in the existing crawlspace. In 1976, Walter Goodman wrote in ''[[The New York Times]]'' that, \"the film presents [the Beales] as a pair of grotesques,\" and asked, \"why were they put on exhibition this way?\"<ref name=\"nyt-goodman\">{{cite news| url=https://www.nytimes.com/1976/02/22/archives/grey-gardens-cinema-verite-or-sideshow-cin-ma-verit-or-sideshow.html | title='Grey Gardens': Cin\u00e9ma Verit\u00e9 or Sideshow? | first=Walter | last=Goodman | newspaper=[[The New York Times]] | date=February 22, 1976 | access-date=March 16, 2021}}</ref> In 2014, in conversation with Alex Simon for ''The Hollywood Interview'', Albert Maysles was asked specifically about the issue of exploitation: \"Grey Gardens was very controversial when it was initially released, with some circles feeling you and David were exploiting these two women who seemed to be mentally ill.\" Maysles replied that, \"as someone with a background in psychology, I knew better than to claim [the Beales] were mentally ill. Their behavior was just their way of asserting themselves. And what could be a better way to assert themselves than a film about them asserting themselves? Nothing more, nothing less. It\u2019s just them. They were always in control.\"<ref>{{cite web |last1=Simon |first1=Alex |title=Albert Maysles: Gimme Some Truth |url=http://thehollywoodinterview.blogspot.com/2014/04/albert-maysles-hollywood-interview.html |website=The Hollywood Interview |access-date=16 March 2021 |date=10 April 2014}}</ref> Nevertheless, the matter has continued to be the subject of debate, with some commentators coming down on the side of exploitation, others on the side of empathy.<ref>{{cite news| url=https://www.metro.us/review-grey-gardens-is-more-empathetic-than-exploitive/ | title=Review: 'Grey Gardens' is more empathetic than exploitive | first=Matt | last=Prigge| newspaper=[[Metro 2021}}</ref> In at least one case, it was concluded that both exploitation and empathy were to be found in the film.<ref>{{cite news| last=Gleiberman| newspaper=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]| date=24 May 2018| access-date=16 March 2021}}</ref>\n\nIn his article, Goodman also pointed out that the Beales were represented in
