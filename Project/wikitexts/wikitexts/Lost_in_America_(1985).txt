The film stars Brooks alongside [[Julie Hagerty]] as a married couple who decide to quit their jobs and travel across America.\n\n==Plot==\nDavid and Linda Howard are typical 1980s [[yuppies]] in [[Los Angeles]], [[California]], dissatisfied with their [[bourgeois]] lifestyle.<ref name= criterion>{{cite web| url= https://www.criterion.com/current/posts/4760-lost-in-america-the-100-000-box |title= Lost in America: The $100,000 Box| first= Scott |last= Tobias |publisher= [[Criterion Collection]]| website= criterion.com | date= July 25, 2017| access-date= February 24, 2021}}</ref> He works in an advertising agency and she for a department store, but after failing to receive an expected promotion and instead being asked to transfer to the firm's [[New York City|New York]] office, David angrily insults his boss and is fired. David coaxes his wife to quit her job as well and seek a new adventure.\n\nThe Howards decide to sell their house, liquidate their assets, drop out of society, \"like in ''[[Easy Rider]]''\", and travel the country in a [[Winnebago Industries|Winnebago]] [[recreational vehicle]].<ref name= criterion /> They leave LA with US$100,000 but their plans change drastically when Linda loses all their savings playing [[roulette]] at the [[Desert Inn]] [[Casino]] in [[Las Vegas, Nevada|Las Vegas]], where David desperately and unsuccessfully persuades a casino manager to give the money back as a publicity gimmick.\n\nWith nowhere to go, the couple quarrels at [[Hoover Dam]], eventually arriving in small-town [[Safford, Arizona|Safford]], [[Arizona]].<ref name= criterion /> David unsuccessfully applies for a delivery job at a local pharmacy and resorts to an employment agency. After a counselor obnoxiously reminds him that he was fired from his high-paying job in advertising, David accepts the best position available \u2014 as a crossing guard, taunted by local schoolchildren.<ref name= criterion /> Linda, meanwhile, finds employment as the assistant manager at the local [[Wienerschnitzel|Der Wienerschnitzel]], working under a person half her age.\n\nOnly a few days after beginning their pursuit of the dream of dropping out of society, David and Linda are living in a [[trailer park]],<ref name= criterion /> nearly broke, and working [[dead-end job]]s where they are accountable to brats. They decide that it is better to return to their old lifestyle as soon as possible. They point the Winnebago toward New York, where David begs for his old job back. The site's consensus states: \"A satire of the American fantasy of leaving it all behind, Lost in America features some of Albert Brooks' best, most consistent writing and cultural jabs.\"<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/lost_in_america|title=Lost in America| website=Rottentomatoes.com| access-date=5 April 2022}}</ref> The film was a commercial success, though not a blockbuster. The film's script won the [[National Society of Film Critics]] award for Best Screenplay.\n\nFilm critic [[Roger Ebert]] gave it 4 out of 4 stars calling it observant and very funny.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.rogerebert.com/reviews/lost-in-america-1985|title=Lost in America movie review|website=Rogerebert.com|access-date=4 March 2021}}</ref>\n\nThe film is #80 on [[Bravo (US TV channel)|Bravo's]] 100 Funniest Movies,<ref>{{cite web |last= |first= |title=BRAVO 100 Funniest Movies |url=https://thefilmspectrum.com/rankings/bravo-100-funniest-movies |website=The Film Spectrum |access-date=19 January 2022}}</ref> and was listed at #84 on [[American Film Institute]]'s [[AFI's 100 Years...100 Laughs]] in 2000.<ref>{{cite web|last= |first= |title=AFI's 100 Years...100 Laughs |url= http://www.afi.com/Docs/100Years/laughs100.pdf |publisher=[[American Film Institute]] |date= |access-date=21 August 2016}}</ref>\n\n==Home media==\n{{unreferenced section|date = July 2022}}\n[[Warner Home Video]] initially released the film on [[Betamax]], [[VHS]], and [[Laserdisc]] in 1985 and reissued it twice on videotape, in 1991 and 1997.
