The title refers to the [[World War II]]-era [[Why We Fight|eponymous propaganda films]] commissioned by the [[U.S. Government]] to justify their decision to enter the war against the [[Axis Powers]].<ref>{{IMDb title |0436971|Why We Fight}}</ref>\n\n''Why We Fight'' was first screened at the [[Sundance Film Festival]] on January 17, 2005, exactly forty-four years after President [[Dwight D. Eisenhower]]'s [[wikisource:Eisenhower's farewell address|farewell address]]. Although it won the Grand Jury Prize for Documentary, the film received a [[limited release|limited public cinema release]] on January 22, 2006. It also won one of the 2006 [[Adolf Grimme Award|Grimme Awards]] in the competition \"Information & Culture\"; the prize is one of Germany's most prestigious for television productions<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.grimme-institut.de/html/index.php?id=66 | year = 2006 | publisher = Grimme Institut | place = DE | access-date = 2013-03-09 | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20140531141946/http://www.grimme-institut.de/html/index.php?id=66%20 | title = Grimme-Preis%20& | archive-date = May 31, 2014 | url-status = dead | df = mdy-all }}</ref> and a [[Peabody Award]] in 2006.<ref>[http://www.peabodyawards.com/award-profile/why-we-fight 66th Annual Peabody Awards], May 2007.</ref>\n\n==Synopsis==\n''Why We Fight'' describes the rise and maintenance of the United States [[military\u2013industrial complex]] and its 50-year involvement with the wars led by the United States to date, especially its [[2003 invasion of Iraq]]. The documentary asserts that in every decade since World War II, the American public was misled so that the government (incumbent [[Executive branch of the United States|Administration]]) could take them to war and fuel the military-industrial economy maintaining American political dominance in the world. Interviewed about this matter are politician [[John McCain]], political scientist and former [[CIA]] analyst [[Chalmers Johnson]], politician [[Richard Perle]], [[Neoconservatism|neoconservative]] commentator [[William Kristol]], writer [[Gore Vidal]], and public policy expert [[Joseph Cirincione]].\n\n''Why We Fight'' documents the consequences of said foreign policy with the stories of a [[Vietnam War]] veteran whose son was killed in the [[September 11, 2001 attacks]], and who then asked the military to write the name of his dead son on any bomb to be dropped in Iraq; a 23-year-old [[New York City|New York]]er who enlists in the [[United States Army]] because he was poor and in debt, his decision impelled by his mother's death; and a military explosives scientist ([[Nguyet Anh Duong|Anh Duong]]) who arrived in the U.S. as a [[refugee]] child from [[Vietnam]] in 1975.\n\n==Producer's list==\nThe producer's list included \"more than a dozen organizations, from the [[Canadian Broadcasting Corporation]] to the United Kingdom's BBC, Estonia's ETV and numerous European broadcasters\" but no U.S. names.<ref name =\"Television Week\">{{Citation | title = Why We Fight | last = Jensen | first = Elizabeth | newspaper = Television Week | date = June 4, 2007 | volume = 26 | issue = 23}}.</ref> The [[Sundance Institute]] did, however, provide completion funding.<ref name = \"Television Week\"/> Writer and director Jarecki said \"serious examination of Eisenhower and the aftermath of his speech proved 'too radical' for potential American funders for his film\" and except for Sundance, he \"could not raise a dollar in the U.S.\"<ref name =\"Television Week\" />\n\n==Contributors and representatives==\n\n===Politicians===\n* Senator [[John McCain]]: elected to the [[United States Senate]] in 1986, he is a former [[Early life and military career of John McCain|U.S. Navy pilot and Vietnam prisoner of war]].\n* [[Richard Perle]], Chairman, Pentagon Defense Policy Board (2001\u201303): worked the U.S. Government for three decades, and is an architect of the G.
