The film's crew consisted of the subjects themselves, who rigged up makeshift lighting and [[Camera dolly|dollies]] and learned to use a [[16mm]] camera with black-and-white film.<ref name=\"austin\" /><ref name=\"indiewire\" />\n\nDuring filming, [[Amtrak]] announced it would be forcibly evicting the people living in the tunnels in order to reroute their trains through the tunnel. This announcement, plus the police presence backing the decision, prompted Singer and photographer [[Margaret Morton (photographer)|Margaret Morton]] to go to the [[Coalition for the Homeless]] for help. Eventually, Singer and Morton managed to secure housing vouchers from the [[Department of Housing and Urban Development]] for the film's subjects, which enabled them to move out of the tunnels and into their own apartments.<ref name=\"austin\" /><ref name=\"indiewire\" />\n\nThe [[post-production]] process for the film took years, with delays caused by financial difficulties and Singer's insistence on creative control to protect the tunnel residents.<ref name=\"austin\" /><ref name=\"indiewire\" /> Melissa Neidich was the editor of the film.\n\n==Soundtrack==\nThe film features music by [[DJ Shadow]].<ref name=\"telegraph\" /> Included in the score are excerpts from ''[[Endtroducing.....]]'' and his [[Psyence Fiction|album]] with [[Unkle]], as well as original music he composed for the main theme of the film, which was released on the single \"Dark Days\".<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://ringostrack.com/en/movie/dark-days/16239 |title=Dark Days (2000) Soundtrack |website=RingosTrack |language=en |access-date=14 June 2017}}</ref>\n\n==Release and reception==\n''Dark Days'' was released in 2000.
