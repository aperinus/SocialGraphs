British six-part spy drama by the [[BBC]]. Directed by [[Simon Langton (television director)|Simon Langton]] and produced by [[Jonathan Powell (producer)|Jonathan Powell]], it is the television adaptation of the 1979 [[spy fiction|spy novel]] ''[[Smiley's People]]'' by [[John le Carr\u00e9]], and a sequel to ''[[Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy (TV series)|Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy]]'' (missing out the second book in the trilogy, ''[[The Honourable Schoolboy]]'', which was not filmed for cost reasons). Starring [[Alec Guinness]], [[Michael Byrne (actor)|Michael Byrne]], [[Anthony Bate]] and [[Bernard Hepton]], it was first shown in the United Kingdom from 20 September to 25 October 1982, and in the United States beginning on 25 October 1982.\n\n==Plot==\n[[George Smiley]] is called out of retirement when one of his former assets, an \u00e9migr\u00e9 general, is found murdered. In tidying up loose ends for the \"[[Secret Intelligence Service|Circus]]\", his former employer, he discovers a clandestine operation run by his nemesis, [[Karla (character)|Karla]], for his own personal benefit. Smiley is able to use this irregularity against Karla, forcing him to defect to [[Western world#Cold War context|the West]].\n\n==Cast==\n{{divcol}}\n*[[Alec Guinness]] as George Smiley\n*[[Eileen Atkins]] as Madame Ostrakova\n*[[Michael Byrne (actor)|Michael Byrne]] as Peter Guillam\n*[[Bernard Hepton]] as Toby Esterhase\n*[[Anthony Bate]] as Sir Oliver Lacon\n*[[Barry Foster (actor)|Barry Foster]] as Sir Saul Enderby\n*[[Michael Lonsdale]] as Anton Grigoriev\n*[[Beryl Reid]] as Connie Sachs\n*[[Bill Paterson (actor)|Bill Paterson]] as Lauder Strickland\n*[[Si\u00e2n Phillips]] as Ann Smiley\n*[[Mario Adorf]] as Claus Kretschmar\n* Tessy Kuhls as Frau Kretzschmar\n*[[Patrick Stewart]] as Karla\n*[[Curd J\u00fcrgens]] as Gen. Due to the success of ''Tinker, Tailor, Soldier, Spy'', director [[John Irvin]] had begun making movies and was unavailable. Later in pre-production, [[Simon Langton (television director)|Simon Langton]] replaced John MacKenzie, Irvin's initial successor as director of the series. During filming, Guinness was not satisfied with Langton, writing; \"I felt dubious about Simon's work... I greatly miss John Irvin's grip and inner tension.\" Arthur Hopcraft declined the offer to write the screenplay and was replaced by [[John Hopkins (screenwriter)|John Hopkins]].<ref name=\"AG\">{{cite book | last = Read | first = Piers Paul | title = Alec Guinness: The Authorised Biography | publisher = Simon & Schuster | year = 2005 | location = Chicago | url = https://archive.org/details/alecguinnessauth00read/page/522 | page = [https://archive.org/details/alecguinnessauth00read/page/522 522] | isbn = 9780743244985 | url-access = registration }}</ref> Author le Carr\u00e9 was called in to help resolve technical difficulties within the script.
