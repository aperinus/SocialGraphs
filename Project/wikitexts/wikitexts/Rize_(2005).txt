It documents the culture and competition surrounding two dance forms known as [[Clown|Clowning]] and [[Krumping]].<ref name=\":0\">{{Cite news|url=http://myartistslist.com/david-lachapelle-american-commercial-photographer-fine-art-photographer-music-video-director-film-director-and-artist/|title=David LaChapelle: American commercial photographer, fine-art photographer, music video director, film director, and artist. - MyArtistsList|date=2015-04-05|newspaper=MyArtistsList|language=en-US|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref>\n\n==Synopsis==\n''Rize'' is a documentary following an interview schedule of two related dancing [[subculture]]s of Los Angeles called [[Krumping#clown|Clowning]] and [[Krumping]].<ref name=\":0\"/> The first series of interviews introduces, describes and develops the dance style known as Clowning.<ref name=\":0\" /> A descendant of 1980s [[breakdancing]], Clowning is a contemporary street art all its own, characterized by speedy, flowing limbs, feverish shakes, hipness, and confounding athletic tricks.<ref name=\"articles.chicagotribune.com\">{{Cite news|url=http://articles.chicagotribune.com/2005-06-24/entertainment/0506240332_1_clowning-rize-freak-shooting|title='Rize' shows the strengths, limits of art to bring hope|newspaper=tribunedigital-chicagotribune|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref> Tommy Johnson, better known by his alias, Tommy the Clown, is a former drug dealer and a man with a mission. For Tommy, Clowning is more than an aesthetic pastime: In an area besieged by drive-by shootings, drug deals and unemployment; Clowning is his way of offering an optimistic alternative for youngsters, a means of self-expression and a chance to channel positive energy.<ref name=\"articles.chicagotribune.com\"/>\n\nThe second series of interviews and footage explains how the dance style known as Krumping evolved from Clowning and matured into its own identity.<ref name=\":0\" /> Like Clowning, Krumping is characterized by free, expressive exaggerated, and highly energetic movement.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.thefreelibrary.com/Getting+krumped:+the+changing+race+of+hip+hop.-a0118675205|title=Getting krumped: the changing race of hip hop. - Free Online Library|website=www.thefreelibrary.com|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref> The youths who started Krumping, known as Lil C' and Miss Prissy, saw the dance as a way for them to escape gang life and \"to release anger, aggression and frustration positively, in a non-violent way.\"\n\nThe third section of the film depicts a dance battle called The Battle Zone which takes place between Clowns and Krumpers at the [[The Forum (Inglewood)|Great Western Forum]] in 2004.<ref name=\":0\" /><ref name=\"Trebay\">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2005/06/19/magazine/the-clowning-wildingout-battle-dancers-of-south-central-la.html|title=The Clowning, Wilding-Out Battle Dancers of South Central L.A.|last=Trebay|first=Guy|date=2005-06-19|newspaper=The New York Times|issn=0362-4331|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref> The film style and soundtrack draws creative ties between African dance rituals and the developing style of Krumping.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.laweekly.com/news/dancing-on-live-grenades-2140132|title=Dancing on Live Grenades|last=Hardy|first=Ernest|date=2005-06-16|newspaper=L.A. Weekly|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref>\n\n== Production ==\nThe film ''Rize'' was written and directed by David LaChapelle. Working alongside LaChapelle were executive producers Ishbel Whitaker, Barry Peele, Ellen Jacobson-Clarke, Stavros Merjos, and Rebecca Skinner.<ref name=\":0\" /> ''Rize'' focuses on the African-American communities of Clowns and Krumpers in [[South Los Angeles|South Central Los Angeles]].<ref name=\":1\">{{Cite web|url=http://www.gnovisjournal.org/2010/11/30/krump-or-die-krumping-and-racist-ideologies-production-and-reception-rize/|title=Krump or Die: Krumping and Racist Ideologies in the Production and Reception of Rize {{!}} gnovis|website=www.gnovisjournal.org|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref> Most of these dancers are young, poor, and would be classified as \"at risk.\" Director David LaChapelle follows these dancers from rehearsal to Battlezone, an annual dance competition, alternating between footage of the dancing and interviews with the dancers and their families.<ref name=\":1\" />\n\nThe film is set in the outskirts of South Central Los Angeles\u2013areas like Inglewood and Compton that have become synonymous in the American popular imagination with deviances of all kinds, due to representations in news media, music, and film. However, LaChapelle does not begin the film in the present day. Focusing instead on the history of racial conflict in South Central, including footage of the Watts riots of 1965 and the 1992 Rodney King riots.<ref name=\":0\" /><ref name=\":1\"/>\n\nLaChapelle situates his film deliberately within a racially specific violence.<ref name=\":1\" /> The film is dedicated to a dancer named Quinesha (Lil Dimples) Dunford, who was killed with a 13-year old friend in a 2003 drive-by shooting, and not one of the dancers within the film is without a story like Quinesha's to tell.<ref name=\"Trebay\"/>\n\n== Soundtrack ==\n*''[[Rize (soundtrack)]]''\nThe documentary features original material by Flii Stylz (\"Rize\", \"I Krump\", \"Beastly\", and \"Recognize\"), Christina Aguilera (\"Soar\"), and an entry by UK hip-hop star Dizzee Rascal (\"Fix Up, Look Sharp\"), as well as a number of traditional gospel songs, including the Edwin Hawkins Singers' \"Oh Happy Day\".<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.allmusic.com/album/rize-mw0000345260|title=Rize - Original Soundtrack {{!}} Songs, Reviews, Credits {{!}} AllMusic|website=AllMusic|access-date=2016-10-18}}</ref>\n\n== Reception ==\n''Rize'' received positive reviews from critics.
