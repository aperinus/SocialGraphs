The film is based on the 1925 [[romance novel]] ''The Sons of the Sheik'' by [[Edith Maude Hull]], and is a sequel to the 1921 hit film ''[[The Sheik (film)|The Sheik]]'', which also stars Rudolph Valentino.<ref name=maccaffrey>{{cite book|editor1=MacCaffrey, Donald W.|editor2=Jacobs, Christopher P.|title=Guide To the Silent Years Of American Cinema|year=1999|publisher=Greenwood Publishing Group|isbn=0-313-30345-2|page=[https://archive.org/details/guidetosilentyea00mcca/page/247 247]|url-access=registration|url=https://archive.org/details/guidetosilentyea00mcca/page/247}}</ref> ''The Son of the Sheik'' is Valentino's final film and went into general release nearly two weeks after his death from [[peritonitis]] at the age of 31.\n\nIn 2003, the film was selected for preservation in the United States [[National Film Registry]] by the [[Library of Congress]] as being \"culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant\".<ref ==\n[[File:The Son of the Sheik (1926).webm|thumb|The full, public domain film]]\nIn his final film performance before dying in 1926, Rudolph Valentino tackles two roles, as a father and his son. Ahmed ([[Rudolph Valentino]]), the son of an [[Arabs|Arab]] [[Sheikh|sheik]] and a kidnapped English gentlewoman ([[Agnes Ayres]]), loves local dancing girl Yasmin ([[Vilma B\u00e1nky|Vilma Banky]]). When he slips out of his father's heavily guarded compound to woo her, he is kidnapped and held for ransom by a group of [[Banditry|bandits]] led by Yasmin's father ([[George Fawcett]]) and Ghabah ([[Montagu Love]]), the Moor to whom she is betrothed.\n\n==Cast==\n* [[Rudolph Valentino]] as Ahmed (The Son) / The Sheik (Ahmed's Father)\n* [[Vilma B\u00e1nky]] as Yasmin\n* [[George Fawcett]] as Andre\n* [[Montagu Love]] as Ghabah\n* [[Karl Dane]] as Ramadan\n* [[Bull Montana]] as Ali the Mountebank\n* Bynunsky Hyman as Pincher the Mountebank\n* [[Agnes Ayres]] as Diana, The Sheik's Wife (Ahmed's Mother)\n\n==Production==\nAt the time of the film's release, Rudolph Valentino was attempting to make a comeback in films.<ref name=dimare>{{cite book|last=DiMare|first=Philip C. |title=Movies in American History: An Encyclopedia, Volume 1|volume=1|year=2011|publisher=ABC-CLIO|isbn=978-1-598-84296-8|page=830}}</ref> He rose to international stardom after the release of ''[[The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse (film)|The Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse]]'' and ''[[The Sheik (film)|The Sheik]]'' in 1921, both of which were box-office hits and solidified his image as \"the Great Lover\".<ref>{{cite book|last=Sandburg|first=Carl |editor=Bernstein, Arnie |others=Ebert, Roger|title=\"The Movies Are\": Carl Sandburg's Film Reviews and Essays, 1920-1928|year=2000|publisher=Lake Claremont Press|isbn=1-893-12105-4|page=309}}</ref> By 1924, however, Valentino's popularity had begun to wane after he appeared in two box office failures, ''[[Monsieur Beaucaire (1924 film)|Monsieur Beaucaire]]'' and ''[[A Sainted Devil]]'', both of which featured him in roles that were a departure from his \"Great Lover\" image. He also squabbled over money with [[Famous Players-Lasky]], the studio he was signed to, which eventually led to him walking out on his contract. Famous Players-Lasky eventually released Valentino from his contract and he signed with [[United Artists]] in 1925.<ref name=dimare /> In an effort to capitalize on the success that Valentino had achieved with ''The Sheik'', United Artists' president [[Joseph M. Schenck]] bought the rights to [[Edith Maude Hull]]'s novel ''Son of the Sheik'' and cast Valentino in the [[dual role]] of father and son.<ref name=maccaffrey /><ref>{{cite book|last1=Block|first1=Alex Ben |last2=Wilson|first2=Lucy Autrey|title=George Lucas's Blockbusting: A Decade-by-Decade Survey of Timeless Movies Including Untold Secrets of Their Financial and Cultural Success|year=2010|publisher=HarperCollins|isbn=978-0-061-96345-2|page=74}}</ref>\n\nThe novel was adapted for the screen by [[Frances Marion]] and [[Fred de Gresac]].<ref name=maccaffrey /> The film was shot on location in [[California]] and in the [[Yuma Desert]] in [[Arizona]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Leider|first=Emily W. |title=Dark Lover: The Life and Death of Rudolph Valentino|year=2004|publisher=Macmillan|isbn=0-571-21114-3|page=369}}</ref>\n\n==Reception==\n{{expand section|date=December 2016}}\n\n===Box office===\n''The Son of the Sheik'' opened at the [[Million Dollar Theater]] in [[Los Angeles]] on July 9, 1926<ref>Leider 2004 p.370</ref> and played for four weeks.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://archive.org/stream/variety84-1926-08#page/n51/mode/1up|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|title='Padlocked,' $32,000, Met's Best'; 'Ben-Hur,' 20 Weeks in L.A.|page=4|date=August 11, 1926|accessdate=May 28, 2018}}</ref>\n\nValentino then embarked on a nationwide tour to promote the film as it rolled out around the first run theatres in the country's cities. On August 15, he collapsed in his New York City hotel room and was rushed to the hospital. Doctors discovered he had a [[perforated ulcer]] which required emergency surgery. After the surgery, Valentino developed [[peritonitis]] and died on August 23, 1926.<ref>{{cite book|last=Eagan|first=Daniel|title=America's Film Legacy: The Authoritative Guide to the Landmark Movies in the National Film Registry|year=2010|publisher=Continuum International Publishing Group|isbn=978-0-826-42977-3|page=[https://archive.org/details/americasfilmlega0000eaga/page/117 117]|url=https://archive.org/details/americasfilmlega0000eaga/page/117}}</ref>\n\n''The Son of the Sheik'' was put into general release nationwide on September 5, 1926, nearly two weeks after Valentino's death. The film grossed $1,000,000 within the first year of its release. Eventually it more than doubled that.<ref name=\"balio ua\">{{cite book|last=Balio|first=Tino|title=United Artists, Volume 1, 1919\u20131950: The Company Built by the Stars|year=2009|publisher=Univ of Wisconsin Press|isbn=978-0-299-23003-6|page=56}}</ref>\n\n===Critical===\nSome critics have heralded Valentino's performance in the film as one of the best of his career.<ref>{{cite
